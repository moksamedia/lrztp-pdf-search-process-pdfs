Overview of the different uses of the auxiliaries of probability
The auxiliaries of probability can be used in either of two ways:
1.

together with “to have” and “to be’;

2.

together with (or as) an auxiliary, after any full or secondary verb.

For example:

ཁོང་བོད་པ་ཡིན་གྱི་རེད།
She might be Tibetan. (mere guessing)
(after "to be")
ཁོང་བོད་ཇ་མང་པོ་འཐུང་གི་ཡོད་འགྲོའོ།
She might drink a lot of Tibetan tea. (mere guessing)
(after full verb + Present auxiliary)

འཕྲིན་ལས་ཞོགས་གས་ནང་ལ་སླེབས་ཡོད་པ་འདྲ།
Seems like Trinley had arrived at home in the morning. (some evidence)
(after full verb + Past auxiliary)

དོན་གྲབ་ཚོགས་འདུ་ལ་ཡོང་ས་མ་རེད།
Looks like Dondrub is not coming to the meeting. (some evidence)
(after a full verb only, see explanation below)

ཁོང་རྒྱ་གར་ལ་ཕེབས་ཐུབ་ས་མ་རེད།
Seems like she is unable to go to India. (some evidence)
(after a secondary verb)
