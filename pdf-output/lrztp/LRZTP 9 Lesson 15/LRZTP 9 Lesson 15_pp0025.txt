a) The auxiliary འགོའ།ོ (also spelled: འགོ།)
This auxiliary expresses a certain probability which is the speaker's opinion, estimation or
guess. The statement is not based on any concrete evidence at the moment of speech.
Examples with "to be" and "to have":
ཁོང་བོད་པ་ཡིན་འགོའ།ོ
Maybe she is Tibetan.
མོ་རང་རྒྱ་གར་བ་མིན་འགོའ།ོ
She is probably not Indian.
སེན་པ་ལ་དངུལ་མང་པོ་ཡོད་འགོའ།ོ
Penpa possibly has lots of money.
སོབ་ཚན་འདི་དཀའ་ལས་ཁག་པོ་མེད་འགོའ།ོ This lesson is probably not difficult. / I wonder,
if this lesson may be hard?
ཁོང་ལ་ཁང་པ་ཆེན་པོ་གསུམ་མེད་འགོའ།ོ
He probably does own three big houses! / I
wonder…
Important side note:
སོབ་ཚན་འདི་དཀའ་ལས་ཁག་པོ་མེད་འགོའ།ོ
ཁོང་ལ་ཁང་པ་ཆེན་པོ་གསུམ་མེད་འགོའ།ོ
In these last two examples, མེད་འགོའ།ོ indicates that it probably is the case, whilst retaining a
sense of question on the part of the speaker. However, predictably, མེད་འགོའོ། can also
sometimes mean "probably not". The safest way to distinguish between the two is by context
and tone of voice. In any case, be aware of these two possible interpretations of མེད་འགོའོ།.
Examples with full and secondary verbs
དེ་རིང་ཟླ་བ་འཛིན་གྲྭ་ལ་ཕེབས་ཀི་མེད་འགོའ།ོ
Dawa is probably not coming to class today. (Present tense)
ཁོང་གིས་རང་སོང་ཚང་མ་དགེ་རྒན་ལ་སད་ཡོད་འགོའ།ོ
He may have given all the homework to the teacher. (Present Perfect)
སོབ་ཕྲུག་ཚོ་ལྷོད་ལྷོད་བེད་འདོད་ཡོད་འགོའ།ོ
The students probably want to relax. (Present tense, secondary verb)
ཁོང་བདུན་ཕག་རེས་མར་ཡང་ཕེབས་ཀི་མིན་འགོའ།ོ Maybe he won't come next week either.
(Future tense)
