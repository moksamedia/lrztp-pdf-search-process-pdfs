3. Fruition verbs, such as ཤེས་པ་ (to know), ཐུབ་པ་ (to be able to) and ལོན་པ་ (to pass an exam) are also བེད་མེད་ལས་ཚིག.
As the name tells, they are the fruition of a process of training – and at this stage, no more volition is involved.
4. Verbs of sensing, such as མཐོང་བ་ (to see), གོ་བ་(to hear) and ན་བ་(to be sick, to hurt) are བེད་མེད་ལས་ཚིག because
there is no control over them.
5. Natural verbs, such as ཤི་བ་ (to die) and སེ་བ་ (to be born, to arise) are བེད་མེད་ལས་ཚིག because they happen
naturally.

བེད་ས་ The Agentive Particle
The ལ་དོན་and the འབེལ་ས་ particles are not too foreign to our ears because they have an equivalent in English.
Conversely, the agentive particle བེད་ས་, does not have an English equivalent and is therefore a bit harder to get used to.
The agentive particle བེད་ས་ is used in Tibetan in order to mark the doer and/or the instrument of an action.
The Various Spellings of the བེད་ས་ Particles:
The བེད་ས་consists of a འབེལ་ས་ with a ས་added. It undergoes the same rules of joining to the preceding word as the འབེལ་ས་. It is
therefore also a ཕྲད་གཞན་དབང་ཅན། dependent particle.

Usage with བ་བེད་ཐ་དད་པ་ and བ་བེད་ཐ་མི་དད་པ་ Verbs
Usage Rules:
Whilst in literary Tibetan, the བེད་ས་ is used in all three times (past, present and future), the rule for colloquial usage is the
following:
1) In the past tense, the བེད་ས་ should/must be used with བ་བེད་ཐ་དད་པ་ verbs.
2) When it is used in the present/future tense or with བ་བེད་ཐ་མི་དད་པ་ verbs in the past, the བེད་ས་ emphasizes the doer.
